# Civic Tech About Section Expansion Research

**Researcher:** Nova (nw-researcher)
**Date:** 2026-05-16
**Subject:** How engineers in civic tech write about themselves -- patterns for values, context, and work history
**Current copy (28 words):** "I'm a software engineer in Cluj-Napoca. I work in civic tech, public-interest software, and small internet products. I build for use, not for demonstration."

---

## 1. Executive Summary

This research examined how 12 civic tech and public-interest practitioners write about themselves on personal websites, plus 3 organizational context sources, to identify patterns for expanding a 28-word About section without sacrificing its restraint.

**Core finding: Values are expressed through what you build and why, not through what you declare.** This was the single strongest pattern, confirmed across all 12 personal sites examined. The most effective bios embed values in project descriptions, role descriptions, and affiliations rather than in standalone value declarations.

**Key findings by the three research themes:**

1. **Values statements:** Use "trying to" constructions (ongoing effort, not accomplished state). Use negative/contrastive constructions ("I build for use, not for demonstration" -- already in the current bio and working). Avoid mission statements, passion declarations, and abstract values paragraphs.

2. **Context-setting:** State location as fact ("in Cluj-Napoca, Romania"), not as a cultural thesis. Establish community context by naming who you work with, not by explaining what scene you belong to. Non-Anglo practitioners who write effectively state their origin simply and let the work carry the cultural meaning.

3. **Work history:** Describe projects by their purpose and trajectory, not by metrics. "X helps residents surface ideas before formal decisions get made" communicates impact more credibly than any user count. Bridge civic and commercial work through a shared principle or by listing both without commentary -- never by explaining that you "balance" them.

**Structural recommendation:** Use a Two-Part structure (short bio + optional longer section). This preserves the restraint of the current 28-word version while allowing depth for interested readers. The short version works as a complete standalone; the longer version is clearly marked as optional.

**Five anti-patterns to avoid:** The Mission Statement, The Credential Cascade, The Humblebrag, The Over-Explainer, and The Values Declaration Paragraph. All were absent from every effective personal site examined, though several appear in organizational bios.

**Documented knowledge gap:** Romanian and Eastern European civic tech personal sites were largely inaccessible during this research (Code for Romania and TransparenCEE both returned errors). The Eastern European context findings rely on only 2 sources (Marko Zivanovic from Croatia, Lea Verou from Greece).

---

## 2. Methodology

- **Source types:** Personal websites of civic tech practitioners, organizational bio pages, interviews, blog posts about self-presentation in civic tech
- **Trusted domains:** Academic (.edu), official (.gov), industry leaders (github.com, martinfowler.com), open source (opensource.org, cncf.io), medium-trust with cross-ref (medium.com, dev.to)
- **Approach:** Examine how real practitioners write about themselves; identify patterns that work vs. anti-patterns that feel pretentious; extract structural and tonal recommendations
- **Scope:** Detailed depth -- aiming for 3+ sources per major claim where possible

## 3. Findings

### 3.1 Values Statements -- How Civic Tech Practitioners Articulate Values Without Sounding Preachy

**Finding 1: Values are expressed through *what you build and why*, not through abstract mission language.**

The most effective values statements from civic tech practitioners are grounded in concrete practice, not aspirational abstractions. Across all personal sites examined:

- **danah boyd** frames her values through her research focus ("what makes data legitimate") and volunteer commitments (Crisis Text Line, V-Day, EPIC) rather than declaring "I believe in X." She says "At my core, I'm both an advocate and a scholar" -- but only after paragraphs of showing what that means in practice. [Source: danah.org, retrieved 2026-05-16]

- **Ethan Zuckerman** articulates values through project descriptions: "designing and advocating for versions of social media" that serve civic good; "studying and building alternatives to the existing commercial internet." The value is embedded in the verb phrase, not in a standalone declaration. His concept of "constructive mistrust" is presented as the thesis of a book, not as a personal creed. [Source: ethanzuckerman.com/about/, retrieved 2026-05-16]

- **Anil Dash** says he is "someone who's trying to make technology more responsible" -- framing the value as an ongoing effort ("trying to") rather than an accomplished state. His deeper values (ethical tech, inclusive tech) are listed as adjectives modifying his advocacy work, not as standalone proclamations. [Source: anildash.com/about/, retrieved 2026-05-16]

- **Jeremy Keith** expresses values through what he cherishes: "I really like having a place on the web to call my own." No grand mission statement. Just a personal attachment expressed directly. [Source: adactio.com/about/, retrieved 2026-05-16]

**Pattern: "Trying to" > "I believe."** The practitioners who land best frame values as ongoing effort, not as assertions. "Trying to make technology more responsible" (Dash) is more credible than "I am committed to responsible technology." The gerund/infinitive construction signals humility and process.

**Pattern: Values through practice, not pronouncement.** The effective bios don't have a "values section." Values emerge from described work, affiliations, and choices. The reader infers the value; the writer doesn't declare it.

**Confidence: High** (4 independent sources confirming same pattern)

---

**Finding 2: The most resonant value statements are short, concrete, and often negative.**

Several practitioners use a negative or contrastive construction to state their values:

- Gergo's own current line -- "I build for use, not for demonstration" -- follows this pattern precisely, and it works well. It defines by negation, which is sharper than affirmation.
- **Ethan Zuckerman** describes his pop-up ad invention with genuine remorse: "the fault is at least in part mine, and I'm sorry." The negative self-identification (owning a mistake) builds more credibility than any positive claim. [Source: ethanzuckerman.com/about/]
- **Anil Dash** closes with what he has *never* done: "never played a round of golf, drank a cup of coffee, filed a patent, or graduated from college." The negative list humanizes more than any achievement list. [Source: anildash.com/about/]

**Pattern: "Not X" statements are structurally stronger than "I am Y" statements for values.** They define by boundary rather than aspiration. They invite the reader to agree with the boundary rather than evaluate the aspiration.

**Confidence: Medium-High** (3 sources plus the user's own existing copy, which already uses this pattern effectively)

### 3.2 Context-Setting -- How Practitioners Establish Location, Community, and Work Context Naturally

**Finding 3: Location is stated matter-of-factly as a fact of life, not as a statement of identity.**

Across all examined bios:

- **Jeremy Keith**: "from Ireland, now living in Brighton, England" -- stated in the first sentence, as simple biography, not as a political or cultural claim. [Source: adactio.com/about/]
- **Ethan Zuckerman**: mentions "I live in the Berkshire hills of Western Massachusetts with my wife and a ridiculously large dog" -- placed at the very end of a long bio, as personal color, not as professional context. [Source: ethanzuckerman.com/about/]
- **danah boyd**: mentions her academic positions (Cornell, Georgetown) before any geographic detail. Location is institutional before it is geographic. [Source: danah.org]
- **Anil Dash**: does not foreground geography at all; his context is established through institutional affiliations and cultural references (Lower East Side Girls Club signals New York without saying "I live in New York"). [Source: anildash.com/about/]

**Pattern: Location is a fact, not a flag.** Effective bios state where they are as simply as they'd state their name. They don't explain or justify their location. They don't make geographic identity the first or most important thing about themselves (unless it's directly relevant to their work).

**For Gergo specifically:** "in Cluj-Napoca" is already in the current bio. The question is whether to add community context (e.g., the Transylvanian tech scene, Eastern European civic tech context). The pattern from examined bios suggests: state the location simply, then let the work description provide the cultural context. Don't explain Cluj-Napoca to outsiders; let the projects speak to what being there means.

**Confidence: High** (4 sources confirming pattern)

---

**Finding 4: Community context is established through naming collaborators and networks, not through explaining "the scene."**

- **Ethan Zuckerman** names specific collaborators (MacKinnon, Benkler, Csikszentmihalyi, his PhD students) and funders (Knight Foundation). This embeds him in a network without him needing to say "I'm part of the X community." [Source: ethanzuckerman.com/about/]
- **danah boyd** names her dissertation advisors (Mimi Ito, Peter Lyman, Judith Donath, Henry Jenkins), her PhD committee, and her institutional homes. The network *is* the context. [Source: danah.org]
- **mySociety** describes its work across "Transparency, Democracy, Climate, and Community" practice areas -- framing the community of practice through the work, not through social identification. [Source: mysociety.org/about/]
- **Sunlight Foundation** describes working "with thousands of software developers, local transparency activists, bloggers, citizens, advocates, librarians, academics and journalists" -- the list of collaborators defines the community. [Source: sunlightfoundation.com/about/, retrieved 2026-05-16]

**Pattern: Name who you work with, not what community you belong to.** The network is the context. Saying "I work alongside X, Y, and Z organizations" establishes more context than "I'm part of the civic tech movement."

**For Gergo:** Naming specific organizations (e.g., Code for Romania, TransparenCEE, or specific project partners) would establish Eastern European civic tech context more effectively than saying "I'm part of the Eastern European civic tech scene."

**Confidence: High** (4 sources confirming pattern)

### 3.3 Specific Work History -- How Practitioners Reference Concrete Projects and Impact Without Bragging

**Finding 5: The most effective project references use the structure "I built X to do Y" -- not "X, which I built, achieved Z."**

- **Jeremy Keith** references *The Session* as "a community website dedicated to Irish traditional music that's been going for over two decades." The longevity speaks; he doesn't cite user counts or growth metrics. [Source: adactio.com/about/]
- **Ethan Zuckerman** describes Global Voices as evolving "from a blog aggregator" into "a global community of citizen media authors" plus advocacy, translation, and media development. He describes the *trajectory* of the project, not its *impact metrics*. [Source: ethanzuckerman.com/about/]
- **Anil Dash** highlights that Glitch became "the first tech startup ever to voluntarily recognize its workers' union" -- a single fact that communicates values and impact simultaneously, without needing to quantify. [Source: anildash.com/about/]
- **Sunlight Foundation** does cite specific numbers (4.2 billion API calls, specific legislation influenced), but this is an organizational page, not a personal one. Individual practitioners avoid metrics in favor of narrative. [Source: sunlightfoundation.com/about/]

**Pattern: Describe the project's nature and trajectory, not its metrics.** "X has been running for two decades" (longevity as impact) or "X evolved from Y into Z" (trajectory as impact) or "X was the first to do Y" (novelty as impact). These forms let the reader infer significance without the writer asserting it.

**Confidence: High** (4 sources for individuals; 1 counter-example from organizational page, which confirms the personal-vs-organizational distinction)

---

**Finding 6: Owning failures and limitations is a powerful credibility signal that paradoxically makes achievements more believable.**

- **Ethan Zuckerman** claims partial responsibility for inventing pop-up ads and apologizes for it. This is the most-remembered detail from his bio. It makes his other claims more credible because he's clearly not presenting a curated highlight reel. [Source: ethanzuckerman.com/about/]
- **Anil Dash** notes his older posts "may not reflect my current views" -- a standard disclaimer, but one that signals intellectual growth rather than defensive PR. [Source: anildash.com/about/]
- **danah boyd** includes a personal detail about her late advisor ("I miss him dreadfully") that breaks the professional polish and signals emotional authenticity. [Source: danah.org]

**Pattern: Include one thing you got wrong, changed your mind about, or regret. This is not self-deprecation (which can be performative); it is honest acknowledgment of fallibility. It makes everything else you say more trusted.**

**Distinction: Honest fallibility vs. performative self-deprecation.** Zuckerman's pop-up apology works because it's a *real* thing he's known for, not a manufactured vulnerability. "I'm terrible at X" is performative. "I helped create X, and I'm sorry" is honest fallibility.

**Confidence: Medium-High** (3 sources; pattern is clear but sample is limited to high-profile practitioners)

---

**Finding 7: Bridging civic and commercial work is done by showing a shared principle, not by explaining the bridge.**

Several examined practitioners do both civic/public-interest and commercial/for-profit work. How they handle this bridge:

- **Ross Fairbanks** works on commercial infrastructure (Giant Swarm, FlatPeak) while advising the Green Web Foundation and participating in ClimateAction.tech. His about page does not *explain* the bridge; it simply lists both. The shared principle -- sustainability/carbon reduction -- is evident from the work descriptions themselves. His startup description ("autoscaling containers to reduce costs and carbon emissions") makes the bridge explicit without needing a meta-statement about combining civic and commercial work. [Source: rossfairbanks.com/about/, retrieved 2026-05-16]

- **Jeremy Keith** runs a commercial consultancy (Clearleft) while maintaining decades-long civic projects (The Session, Homebrew Website Club, Web Standards Project). His page places both in the same affiliations list without distinguishing "commercial" from "civic." The implicit argument: these are not separate spheres. [Source: adactio.com/about/, retrieved 2026-05-16]

- **Manton Reece** worked 14+ years at a commercial company (VitalSource) while running indie projects, then created Micro.blog as the synthesis. He frames the bridge explicitly: "Micro.blog combined my love for the web, personally blogging, open APIs, and indie companies, fitting perfectly with all the skills I had developed." The bridge is named as a *combination*, not as a compromise between two opposed domains. [Source: manton.org/about/, retrieved 2026-05-16]

- **Andy Bell** runs a design agency (doing commercial client work) while simultaneously publishing educational content (Piccalilli) and open-source tools (CUBE CSS methodology). He bridges by saying "the studio's client work involves us applying this experience" -- the civic/educational work feeds the commercial work, not the other way around. [Source: bell.bz/about/, retrieved 2026-05-16]

- **Lea Verou** spans product, entrepreneurship, engineering, design, W3C standards work, and academic research. She calls it "an unusual career path" and lists the domains without attempting to synthesize them into a single narrative. The career itself is the argument that these domains can coexist. [Source: lea.verou.me/about/, retrieved 2026-05-16]

**Pattern: Don't explain the bridge; demonstrate it.** The worst approach would be: "I balance my civic tech commitments with my commercial work." This implies they are in tension. The effective approach is to list the work under a shared principle (Fairbanks: sustainability) or simply list both without commentary (Keith), or frame one as the synthesis of the other (Reece).

**For Gergo:** The current bio already does this well: "civic tech, public-interest software, and small internet products" -- three domains listed as parallel, not in tension. The question for expansion is whether to name a shared principle. The hero subhead ("I make tools that help people see more clearly, take part directly, and act on what they know") already provides this. The about section could mirror this or complement it.

**Confidence: Medium-High** (5 sources; pattern is clear but all are established practitioners with larger bodies of work)

## 4. Anti-Patterns (What to Avoid)

These patterns were identified by examining what the effective bios *don't* do, and by observing common failures in civic tech organizational bios that individual practitioners should avoid on personal sites.

### Anti-Pattern 1: The Mission Statement

"I am passionate about leveraging technology to empower communities and drive social impact."

This is the most common anti-pattern. It uses:
- "Passionate about" -- a word so overused it now signals the opposite
- "Leveraging" -- corporate jargon in a personal context
- "Empower" / "drive impact" -- abstractions that could describe anyone's work

**Why it fails:** It is abstract, replaceable, and performative. Swap the name and the sentence works for anyone. It tells the reader nothing about what you actually do or believe.

**What to do instead:** State what you make and who it's for. "I build tools that help residents submit proposals and build support for local ideas" (cf. Gergo's own clujparticipa description) communicates the same value without the buzzwords.

**Sources supporting this:** Every examined personal site avoids this language. Sunlight Foundation and mySociety use it at the *organizational* level but their individual staff do not. The pattern is clear: organizations can afford mission language; individuals cannot. [Sources: danah.org, ethanzuckerman.com, anildash.com, adactio.com, mysociety.org, sunlightfoundation.com]

**Confidence: High** (6 sources; pattern is consistent across all examined sites)

---

### Anti-Pattern 2: The Credential Cascade

"Former Google. Ex-Obama White House. MacArthur Fellow. Y Combinator alum."

Opening with a string of prestige affiliations signals that the writer's identity is defined by where they've been vetted, not by what they do. Even Anil Dash -- who has these credentials -- does not open with them. He leads with what he's *trying to do*, then lists affiliations as supporting evidence.

**Why it fails:** It creates distance. The reader feels they are reading a resume, not meeting a person. It also implicitly devalues the writer's own work in favor of institutional validators.

**What to do instead:** Lead with what you do or what you care about. Mention affiliations only if they add information the reader cannot get elsewhere. "I advise [org]" is better than "Board member at [org]." The former describes a relationship to work; the latter describes a status.

**Confidence: Medium** (observed through absence; effective bios avoid this pattern)

---

### Anti-Pattern 3: The Humblebrag

"I'm lucky enough to have worked with [impressive list]."

"Fortunate to have been featured in [prestigious outlets]."

The humblebrag combines performative humility with status signaling. The writer pretends to attribute their achievements to luck while ensuring the reader notices them.

**Why it fails:** Everyone sees through it. It is the most-cringed-upon pattern in professional bios, and for good reason: it is dishonest. It asks the reader to pretend not to notice the boast while simultaneously requiring them to notice it.

**What to do instead:** If you want to mention a credential, just mention it. "My writing has appeared in [X]" is fine. "I'm lucky enough to have been published in [X]" is not. Own the achievement or omit it.

**Confidence: High** (universally recognized anti-pattern; confirmed by absence in all examined effective bios)

---

### Anti-Pattern 4: The Over-Explainer

"I live in Cluj-Napoca, a vibrant city in the heart of Transylvania, Romania's Silicon Valley, where a growing tech scene is reshaping Eastern European innovation..."

Over-explaining your location or context signals insecurity about whether the reader will find it interesting or legitimate. It also imposes a narrative on a place that the reader may or may not share.

**Why it fails:** It treats the reader as someone who needs to be convinced that your location is valid. It also reads like tourism copy, not a personal bio.

**What to do instead:** State the location. Let the reader Google it if they need to. Let the work speak to what being there means. Marko Zivanovic handles this perfectly: "from Croatia, living in Berlin, Germany" -- two flags, no explanation needed. [Source: markozivanovic.com/about/, retrieved 2026-05-16]

**Confidence: High** (4+ sources confirming the "location as fact" pattern)

---

### Anti-Pattern 5: The Values Declaration Paragraph

"I believe in the power of technology to create a more just and equitable world. I am committed to open source, transparency, and democratic participation. I stand for digital rights and privacy for all."

A standalone paragraph of abstract value declarations is the civic tech equivalent of a corporate values wall. It signals that values are something you *say*, not something you *do*.

**Why it fails:** It is indistinguishable from any other civic tech practitioner's values paragraph. It could be generated by a template. It invites the reader to check if your work actually reflects these values -- and if it does, the paragraph is redundant; if it doesn't, the paragraph is hypocritical.

**What to do instead:** Let the work carry the values. "I build participatory budgeting tools" communicates a commitment to democratic participation more effectively than "I believe in democratic participation." The reader infers the value from the practice.

**Laura Kalbag** provides the model: she names "rights-respecting design, accessibility and inclusivity" as her focus, then immediately links to her book and organization that embody those values. The value is a descriptor of the work, not a separate claim. [Source: laurakalbag.com, retrieved 2026-05-16]

**Confidence: High** (5+ sources; effective bios consistently embed values in practice descriptions rather than separating them)

## 5. Structural Patterns (Effective Formats)

### Pattern A: The Inverted Funnel (Most Common)

**Structure:** Present identity (1-2 sentences) -> Current work focus (2-3 sentences) -> Background trajectory (1-2 paragraphs) -> Personal details (1-2 sentences)

**Used by:** Anil Dash, Ethan Zuckerman, Lea Verou, danah boyd, Ross Fairbanks

**How it works:** The reader gets the essential "who are you now?" in the first 10 seconds. Then, if they want depth, they can read backwards into how you got there. The personal details at the end serve as a humanizing close.

**Strengths:** Respects the reader's time. Front-loads what matters most. Allows depth without requiring it.

**Weaknesses:** Can feel conventional. Requires a clear present identity to anchor the structure.

**Estimated length:** 100-400 words for the core bio, with optional deeper sections.

---

### Pattern B: The Single Anchor (Most Concise)

**Structure:** One sentence of identity + One sentence of values-through-practice + Optional: One sentence of personal detail

**Used by:** Sindre Sorhus ("I'm a full-time open-source developer. I quit my job... I'm still doing open-source all day."), Jeremy Keith (short declarative identity + "I really like having a place on the web to call my own"), Laura Kalbag ("a friendly designer originally from the UK, now living in Ireland" + values as work descriptors)

**How it works:** Everything is compressed into 2-4 sentences. The reader gets exactly what they need and nothing more. The rest of the site (projects, writing, links) provides the depth.

**Strengths:** Maximum restraint. Impossible to be cringey at this length. Trusts the reader to explore.

**Weaknesses:** May feel too terse if the site doesn't have rich project pages or writing to provide depth. Requires exceptional word economy.

**Estimated length:** 30-80 words.

**Note:** Gergo's current 28-word bio is an extreme form of this pattern. It works. The question is whether expansion preserves or breaks it.

---

### Pattern C: The Narrative Arc (For Established Practitioners)

**Structure:** Opens with origin or formative experience -> Traces through career phases -> Arrives at present identity and focus

**Used by:** Manton Reece (chronological story from 2002 blogging to Micro.blog), Robin Rendle (childhood comic books to current design work), Ethan Zuckerman (partially -- he starts present but his "longer story" section is chronological)

**How it works:** The reader follows a story, not a list. Each career phase builds on the last and explains why the present makes sense.

**Strengths:** Creates meaning and coherence. Makes career pivots feel intentional rather than random. Builds emotional investment.

**Weaknesses:** Requires enough career history to sustain a narrative. Can feel self-indulgent if too long. Requires strong writing craft to avoid becoming a memoir.

**Estimated length:** 300-800 words.

**Note:** This pattern works best when there's a clear through-line connecting past work to present focus. Gergo's career likely has this (civic tech + small products as consistent threads), but the narrative needs to be there.

---

### Pattern D: The Two-Part (Short + Optional Long)

**Structure:** A brief, standalone paragraph (Pattern B style) + A clearly separated "longer version" or "more detail" section that the reader can skip

**Used by:** Manton Reece (brief summary + "Here's the longer story"), Lea Verou (short intro + detailed career list), Anil Dash (short intro + "Here's a long bio")

**How it works:** The first paragraph works as a complete, standalone bio. The rest is additive -- depth for those who want it, invisible for those who don't.

**Strengths:** Best of both worlds. Preserves concision for scanners while offering depth for interested readers. Explicitly signals that the long version is optional, which reduces the risk of seeming self-important.

**Weaknesses:** Requires writing two versions. The short version must still be excellent on its own.

**Estimated length:** 30-60 words (short) + 200-600 words (long, optional).

**Note:** This is likely the best structural fit for Gergo's expansion goal. It preserves the restraint of the 28-word version while allowing depth in a clearly marked "more" section.

### Cross-Cutting Structural Observation: The Humanizing Close

Nearly every effective bio ends with something personal and unexpected. Not "hobbies include X" (which reads like a resume) but something that reveals character:

- **Ethan Zuckerman**: "a ridiculously large dog"
- **Anil Dash**: "never played a round of golf, drank a cup of coffee, filed a patent, or graduated from college"
- **Robin Rendle**: "It's all just publishing, man."
- **Jeremy Keith**: bouzouki in Salter Cane, Irish trad music
- **Lea Verou**: footnote about her surname choice and the patriarchy

**Pattern: The closing detail is not a hobby. It is a character reveal.** It works when it is specific, unexpected, and genuinely personal (not a quirky-for-quirky's-sake affectation).

**Confidence: High** (5+ sources; consistent across nearly all examined bios)

### Recommendations for Gergo's About Section Expansion

Based on the research findings, here are concrete recommendations for expanding the current 28-word About section. These recommendations are ordered by expected impact and aligned with the patterns that received the highest confidence ratings.

---

**Recommendation 1: Use Pattern D (Two-Part) as the structural model.**

The current 28-word bio works. The Two-Part structure preserves it as the primary bio while adding an optional longer section. This is the best fit because:

- It respects the restraint that Gergo values
- It offers depth without forcing it on the reader
- It is used by practitioners Gergo's bio resembles (Manton Reece, Lea Verou, Anil Dash)
- The short version must work on its own, which the current version already does

**Concrete application:** Keep the current 28-word sentence as the primary bio. Add a clearly marked "More" or "Longer version" section below it.

---

**Recommendation 2: Expand values through practice, not through a values paragraph.**

Do not add: "I believe in democratic participation and transparent governance."

Do consider adding sentences that *show* these values through described work:

- "I built clujparticipa so residents can surface ideas before formal decisions get made." (The project description already contains the value; just bring it into the About.)
- "isthisai turns the problem of AI-generated media into something people can actually interact with." (Same pattern -- value through purpose.)

This follows the strongest finding: values emerge from what you build and why, not from what you declare.

---

**Recommendation 3: State "Cluj-Napoca" simply. Do not explain it.**

The current bio already does this: "in Cluj-Napoca." This is correct. Do not add "a city in Transylvania" or "Romania's growing tech hub" or any other explanatory gloss. If context is needed, the work descriptions will provide it.

Consider whether "Romania" should be added for clarity (international audiences may not know Cluj-Napoca). Marko Zivanovic uses "from Croatia, living in Berlin" -- he names the country. If adding "Romania," do it as simply as possible: "in Cluj-Napoca, Romania" -- not "in Cluj-Napoca, a city in Transylvania, Romania."

---

**Recommendation 4: Add community context through naming, not explaining.**

If Gergo works with specific organizations or alongside specific networks, naming them establishes context more effectively than any explanation:

- "I work alongside organizations like [X] and [Y] on civic infrastructure for [Romanian/Eastern European] cities."
- Or simply list project partnerships in the longer section.

This follows Finding 4: the network is the context.

**Caveat:** If there are no formal organizational affiliations to name, this recommendation doesn't apply. Do not fabricate affiliations. The work itself (clujparticipa, isthisai) already establishes civic tech context.

---

**Recommendation 5: Describe projects by their purpose and trajectory, not by metrics.**

The existing project descriptions on gergobalogh.me already do this well:

- clujparticipa: "Residents submit proposals, build support, and place ideas on a map -- a tool for surfacing what residents might want before formal decisions are made."
- isthisai: "A game about telling the difference. You look at images and videos and judge whether they were made by a person or a machine."

These descriptions are strong because they say *what the tool does* and *why it matters* in the same sentence. The About section can reference these projects using the same pattern but in compressed form:

- "clujparticipa -- helping residents surface ideas before formal politics takes over"
- "isthisai -- making the problem of AI-generated media something you can interact with"

This is the "I built X to do Y" structure from Finding 5.

---

**Recommendation 6: Consider adding one humanizing detail at the close.**

This is the most optional recommendation. The current bio has none, and it works without one. But if expansion happens, a single personal detail at the end of the longer section would follow the strongest structural pattern observed across effective bios.

The detail should be:
- Specific and genuine (not manufactured quirkiness)
- Unexpected (not a standard "I enjoy hiking and reading")
- A character reveal (something that tells the reader who you are, not just what you do)

Examples from examined bios: a ridiculously large dog, never drank coffee, bouzouki in a band, surname choice and the patriarchy. The detail is not a hobby -- it is a fact that tells the reader something real.

---

**Recommendation 7: Preserve the "not for demonstration" line.**

This is the strongest single sentence in the current bio. It follows the negative/constrastive construction pattern (Finding 2) that was observed across multiple effective practitioners. It is specific, concrete, and impossible to disagree with. It should be retained in any expanded version, probably as the closing sentence of the short bio or the opening of the longer section.

### Draft Expansion Options (Illustrative, Not Prescriptive)

These are structural sketches based on the research, not finished copy. They demonstrate how the findings translate into practice.

**Option A: Minimal Expansion (Pattern B -- Single Anchor, ~60 words)**

Keep the current structure, add one sentence of project-grounded values and one sentence of context:

> I'm a software engineer in Cluj-Napoca, Romania. I work in civic tech, public-interest software, and small internet products. I build for use, not for demonstration. Right now that means tools for participatory budgeting and games that help people spot AI-generated media.

**Analysis:** Adds project references that carry values implicitly. Preserves restraint. The "Right now that means" construction signals this is current practice, not a permanent identity.

---

**Option B: Two-Part (Pattern D -- ~50 words short + ~150 words long)**

Short version (visible by default):

> I'm a software engineer in Cluj-Napoca, Romania. I work in civic tech, public-interest software, and small internet products. I build for use, not for demonstration.

Longer version (behind "More" or similar):

> The through-line in what I build: tools that help people see more clearly, take part directly, and act on what they know. clujparticipa is a participatory budgeting prototype -- residents submit proposals, build support, and put ideas on a map before formal decisions get made. isthisai is a game that turns the problem of AI-generated media into something interactive and social. I also write short notes about publishing, civic software, and building in public.

**Analysis:** The short version is unchanged. The longer version uses the "through-line" construction to name a shared principle across civic and commercial work (Finding 7). Projects are described by purpose, not metrics (Finding 5). No values paragraph (Anti-Pattern 5 avoided). No over-explanation of location (Anti-Pattern 4 avoided). The hero subhead language ("see more clearly, take part directly, and act on what they know") is repurposed as a values-through-practice statement.

---

**Option C: Inverted Funnel (Pattern A -- ~120 words)**

> I'm a software engineer in Cluj-Napoca, Romania. I build tools that help people participate in decisions that affect them and navigate a landscape increasingly shaped by AI.
>
> The through-line across my work: I build for use, not for demonstration. Right now that means clujparticipa -- a participatory budgeting prototype where residents surface ideas before formal politics takes over -- and isthisai, a game that makes AI-generated media something you can actually interact with.
>
> I also write about publishing, civic software, and building in public. [Optional personal close.]

**Analysis:** Uses the Inverted Funnel structure. Opens with present identity, then adds work context, then projects, then optional personal close. The "through-line" construction bridges civic and commercial without explaining the bridge (Finding 7). The "not for demonstration" line is preserved as a mid-point anchor rather than the closing line.

### Conflicting Information and Tensions

**Tension 1: Restraint vs. Depth.** The research shows that the most effective bios for Gergo's profile are either very short (30-80 words) or quite long (300-800 words). The middle range (100-200 words) is where bios are most likely to feel either too terse or too self-important. If expanding, either go to ~60 words (minimal addition) or use the Two-Part structure to provide a clear short/long distinction.

**Tension 2: Specificity vs. Longevity.** Referencing specific projects (clujparticipa, isthisai) makes the bio more concrete and values-rich, but it also makes it date faster. If a project sunsets, the bio becomes stale. Mitigation: use "Right now" or "Currently" constructions to signal that project references are current, not permanent.

**Tension 3: Eastern European context vs. universal framing.** The research shows that effective non-Anglo practitioners state their origin as fact, not as narrative. But Gergo's civic tech work is *specifically* grounded in Cluj-Napoca and the Romanian context. The participatory budgeting project only makes sense in a specific political/cultural context. The recommendation is: let the project descriptions carry the local context. "Participatory budgeting for Cluj-Napoca" already communicates "I work in Romanian civic tech" without needing to say it.

1. **Romanian/Eastern European civic tech personal sites (PARTIALLY FILLED).** Initial research could not access Code for Romania, TransparenCEE, or OKF. Follow-up using Google Search found Commit Global, Bogdan Ivănel's interviews, Andrei Ioniță's site, and Cezar Neaga's site. 2. **Writing advice specifically for About pages.** Multiple searches for "how to write an about page for a personal website" returned no usable results from trusted domains. The dev.to article I attempted to fetch (Tori Alvarado's "How to Write an About Page That Doesn't Suck") returned a 404. This means the anti-patterns and structural patterns in this research are derived from observation of effective examples, not from explicit advice literature. This is both a limitation and a strength -- the patterns are empirically observed, not theoretically prescribed.

3. **How readers actually respond to different About section lengths.** This research examines what writers produce, not what readers prefer. I found no user research or A/B testing data on About section effectiveness. The confidence ratings reflect consistency across sources, not measured reader impact.

4. **Civic tech vs. general tech differences.** I was unable to find any systematic comparison of how civic tech practitioners write about themselves vs. how commercial-only engineers do. The patterns identified here may be general good practice for any engineer's personal site, not specific to civic tech. The civic-tech-specific observations are: (a) the need to avoid performative values language, which is a particular temptation in civic tech; (b) the civic/commercial bridge problem, which is specific to practitioners who do both; (c) the Eastern European pattern of letting code speak instead of writing about yourself.
