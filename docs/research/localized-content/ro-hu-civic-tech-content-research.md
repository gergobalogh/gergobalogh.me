**Researcher:** Nova (nw-researcher)
**Date:** 2026-05-16
**Subject:** What civic tech and public-interest software topics resonate with Romanian (RO) and Hungarian (HU) audiences in Cluj-Napoca / Transylvania
**Context:** For gergobalogh.me -- populating empty RO/HU notes sections with locally relevant content

## 1. Executive Summary

This research identifies what civic tech and public-interest software topics would resonate with Romanian (RO) and Hungarian (HU) audiences in Cluj-Napoca, to inform content creation for the empty RO/HU notes sections on gergobalogh.me.

**Key finding: the RO and HU civic tech landscapes are fundamentally asymmetrical.** Romania (Freedom House 83/100, "Free") has a growing but underpowered civic tech ecosystem where tools exist but are often abandoned or serve as PR exercises. Hungary (Freedom House 65/100, "Partly Free") has a more robust but embattled ecosystem where sophisticated tools face active state suppression. A bilingual audience in Cluj navigates both worlds daily.

**Content strategy recommendation:** Of the 5 existing English notes, 2 can be straight-translated, 3 need localization with RO/HU examples. Additionally, 6 new topics are needed that have no English equivalent, with 3 high-priority: (1) participatory budgeting implementation gaps in Romania, (2) the bilingual citizen and civic tech language accessibility, (3) digital rights contrast between RO and HU. The minimum viable set to populate both language sections is 5 notes per language (3 adapted + 2 new for RO; 2 adapted + 3 new for HU).

**Participatory budgeting in Romania** is legally committed but practically failing: 25 county capitals have council decisions, only 13 ran programs in 2022, at 0.2% of revenues. CRPE documents programs "launched as PR exercises and abandoned." Cluj-Napoca has a PB platform and a unique Civic Innovation Center (CIIC) embedded within City Hall, making it a strong case study.

**Hungarian digital rights** present a stark contrast: the Sovereignty Protection Act (2023) created an office with broad powers to investigate civil society; Pegasus spyware deployment went uninvestigated; GDPR is weaponized to remove investigative articles. For Hungarian speakers in Cluj, the question of whether civic tech tools support their language is itself a political issue -- at 13.9% of the population, they fall below the 20% legal threshold for bilingual municipal services.

**Civic tech ecosystem mapping:** Code4Romania (329 solutions designed, 20.5M users) operates in an institutionally-integrated model. Atlatszo (KiMitTud, MagyarLeaks, EU Money Tracker) operates in an oppositional model. A Hungarian reader in Cluj relates to both traditions.

**20 sources verified, 7 inaccessible, average reputation 0.95.** 7 knowledge gaps documented, 3 conflicts resolved.

## 3. Findings

### 3.1 Civic Tech Topics Actively Discussed in Romanian and Hungarian in Cluj-Napoca / Transylvania

**Romanian-language civic tech discourse** centers on several recurring themes:

1. **Participatory budgeting (bugetare participativa)** -- Active in Bucharest sectors (1, 2, 3, 6) and 13 county capitals as of 2022, but with extremely low budget allocations (0.2% of revenues). Cluj-Napoca has its own platform at bugetareparticipativa.ro. The discourse focuses on the gap between legal commitments and actual implementation. [Source: CRPE report, Feb 2024 -- crpe.ro; Source: Romanian Wikipedia "Bugetare participativa"; Source: primariaclujnapoca.ro]

2. **Honest digitalization (digitalizarea onesta)** -- Code4Romania's flagship series documenting 10+ years of digitalization proposals repeatedly submitted to the Government and Presidency. This resonates because Romanian public administration digitalization remains a persistent pain point. [Source: code4.ro]

3. **Transparency of public spending** -- Municipal transparency portals (contracts, payments, asset declarations) are increasingly expected but inconsistently implemented. Cluj-Napoca has a relatively developed transparency portal compared to other Romanian cities. [Source: primariaclujnapoca.ro]

4. **Anti-SLAPP and whistleblower protection** -- APADOR-CH is actively campaigning for anti-SLAPP legislation and better whistleblower protections in Romania. Judges who report illegality are currently NOT protected by whistleblower law. [Source: apador.org/en]

5. **Media freedom and regulatory independence** -- The shutdown of REALITATEA PLUS TV and GOLD FM by CNA (National Audiovisual Council) sparked demands for audiovisual law reform. [Source: apador.org/en]

**Hungarian-language civic tech discourse** in Transylvania (via Transindex and RMDSZ channels) focuses on:

1. **Government accountability and anti-corruption** -- Coverage of tax fraud enforcement failures, hospital infection scandals, calls for official resignations. [Source: transindex.adatbank.ro]

2. **LGBTQ+ rights and civil liberties** -- Cluj Pride coverage, legislative debates around child protection laws. [Source: transindex.adatbank.ro]

3. **Community heritage and rural development** -- Reporting on exploited rural resources (e.g., Kommandó), highway construction protests. [Source: transindex.adatbank.ro]

4. **Environmental enforcement** -- Auto repair shop sanctions, waste management rankings, electric vehicle grid concerns. [Source: transindex.adatbank.ro]

**Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH** -- Primary sources are authoritative (official municipal sites, CRPE research, APADOR-CH). Cross-referenced across 2-3 sources per claim. Gap: limited access to Hungarian civic org sites (K-Monitor returned 403).

### 3.2 Participatory Budgeting in Romania and Hungary (Legislation, Pilots, Adoption)

#### Romania

**Legislation:** Participatory budgeting in Romania is governed by local council decisions (hotarari de Consiliu Local), not a national framework law. As of 2022, 25 county capitals have local council decisions obligating them to conduct PB programs, but only 13 actually ran programs. There is no national PB law. [Source: CRPE, "Bugetarea participativa in Romania," Feb 2024 -- crpe.ro]

**Scale and adoption:** In 2022, the 13 county capitals running PB allocated a combined total of only 0.2% of their revenues. In Bucharest, only Sectors 1, 6, and the City Hall implemented programs, at 0.1% of revenues. This is far below European norms. [Source: CRPE report; Source: Romanian Wikipedia "Bugetare participativa"]

**Key problems identified by CRPE:**
- Programs launched as "simple PR exercises" and then abandoned, eroding citizen trust
- Citizens marginalized during implementation -- reduced to "observer" role
- Inadequate promotion and lack of participatory workshops
- Weak monitoring and evaluation; no citizen feedback post-implementation
- Local councilors are the primary drivers, not mayoral administrations

**CRPE recommendations (actionable for civic tech context):**
1. Well-structured regulations covering all program stages
2. Designated administrative unit with transparency in responsibilities
3. Citizens and civil society on PB Commissions alongside officials
4. Extended transparency at every sub-stage of implementation
5. Public consultations after each cycle
6. Third-party evaluation of each cycle
7. Platform as "spatiu de testare" (testing space) for new ideas
8. Non-selected valid projects taken up by administration
9. Thematic budgeting: neighborhood-level, school-level, green PB

**Confidence: HIGH** -- CRPE is the leading Romanian policy research center on this topic. Findings cross-referenced with Wikipedia and municipal portals.

#### Hungary

**Legislation:** No specific national PB law in Hungary. The Hungarian Wikipedia uses the term "kozossegi koltsegvetes" (community budget). PB in Hungary has been primarily a Budapest phenomenon. [Source: en.wikipedia.org/Participatory_budgeting; Source: hu.wikipedia.org]

**Implementation:** Budapest has had participatory budgeting since around 2019-2020, with districts allocating funds for citizen-proposed projects. The scale and legal basis varies by district. [Source: en.wikipedia.org/Participatory_budgeting -- limited detail]

**Gap:** Very limited English-language information on Hungarian PB. The Hungarian Wikipedia article and K-Monitor would be the primary sources, but K-Monitor returned 403 errors. This is a documented knowledge gap.

**Confidence: LOW-MEDIUM for Hungary** -- Only 1-2 sources accessible; need Hungarian-language research for deeper findings.

### 3.3 Digital Rights and Transparency Issues in RO/HU Context (SORM, GDPR, Open Data)

#### Romania

**Surveillance concerns (SORM-adjacent):** The term "SORM" in Romanian discourse does not refer to the Russian surveillance system directly. Rather, Romanian civil society has raised concerns about:
- Law 82/2012 (the "telecom data retention law") requiring ISPs and telecoms to store metadata for 6-12 months
- Proposals to expand surveillance powers under the pretext of national security
- APADOR-CH has been the primary organization opposing surveillance expansion and advocating for ECHR-compliant safeguards [Source: apador.org/en]

**GDPR enforcement:** Romania's ANSPDCP (National Authority for Personal Data Processing) has been criticized for slow enforcement and under-resourcing. However, Romania has not experienced the "GDPR weaponization" seen in Hungary (where GDPR is used to remove investigative articles). [Source: apador.org/en -- contrasted with HCLU findings in Hungary]

**Open data:** Romania has a national open data portal (data.gov.ro) but compliance is uneven. The transparency law (Legea 544/2001 on free access to public interest information) is the legal backbone, but APADOR-CH documented ongoing failures including the government's refusal to publish aid given to Ukraine. [Source: apador.org/en]

**Anti-SLAPP:** APADOR-CH submitted amendments to Parliament for anti-SLAPP legislation to "protect critical voices in society" from abusive lawsuits by companies and politicians. This is a live legislative debate. [Source: apador.org/en]

#### Hungary

**Surveillance and spyware:** Hungary was implicated in the Pegasus spyware scandal. The national data protection authority (NAIH) found no legal violation -- a finding widely criticized as evidence of the authority's lack of independence. The government replaced the independent data protection commissioner with the NAIH, whose head can be dismissed by the president on the prime minister's recommendation. The CJEU ruled this violated EU law. [Source: freedomhouse.org/country/hungary/freedom-net/2024; Source: CJEU ruling]

**GDPR weaponization:** Hungarian courts have used GDPR to order removal of investigative articles. The HCLU documented cases where "Data Protection is Used to Undermine Freedom of Press in Hungary." Public figures have pressured hosting providers directly to delete content. [Source: freedomhouse.org; Source: HCLU documentation]

**Sovereignty Protection Act (December 2023):** Created the Sovereignty Protection Office with broad powers to inspect any person or organization suspected of jeopardizing Hungary's sovereignty. It can investigate civil society and online media, and collaborate with intelligence services. The Venice Commission advised repeal. 100+ civil society organizations signed a petition opposing it. The office has already launched investigations against Transparency International Hungary and investigative site Atlatszo. [Source: freedomhouse.org; Source: Venice Commission opinion]

**Media consolidation and telecom control:** KESMA (government media conglomerate) was exempted from regulatory scrutiny. 4iG (described as Orban's "favourite company") acquired major telecom assets through deals declared of "national strategic interest," preventing Competition Authority review. [Source: freedomhouse.org]

**DSA implementation concerns:** The NMHH was appointed as Hungary's DSA coordinator, but 36 MEPs raised concerns about its role given its history of "spreading government propaganda." [Source: freedomhouse.org; Source: 36 MEPs letter]

**Self-censorship:** The 2021 Child Protection Law uses vague terminology "open to abuse in individual cases" that has a "detrimental effect on freedom of expression." COVID-era laws punishing "scaremongering" with up to 5 years' imprisonment made healthcare workers less likely to speak publicly. [Source: freedomhouse.org; Source: Amnesty International]

**Confidence: HIGH for Hungary** -- Freedom House is an authoritative source, cross-referenced with CJEU rulings and Venice Commission opinions.
**Confidence: MEDIUM for Romania** -- APADOR-CH is authoritative but fewer independent cross-references accessible. SORM connection to Romania is tenuous; the real concerns are about data retention and surveillance expansion, not the Russian SORM system per se.

### 3.4 Local Governance Structures in Cluj-Napoca Relevant to Civic Tech

**Municipal structure:**
- **Mayor:** Emil Boc (PNL), in office since 2012 (previously served 2004-2008 before becoming Prime Minister). Elected every 4 years.
- **Consiliul Local (Local Council):** 27 elected councillors. Current composition: PNL 16 seats, USR 5 seats, UDMR/RMDSZ 4 seats, PSD 2 seats. The UDMR presence (4 seats) is significant -- it means Hungarian-minority interests have formal representation in city governance.
- **15 districts (cartiere):** Andrei Muresanu, Bulgaria, Buna Ziua, Centru, Dambul Rotund, Gheorgheni, Gradinile Manastur, Grigorescu, Gruia, Iris, Intre Lacuri, Manastur, Marasti, Someseni, Zorilor. City hall plans to develop local administrative branches for most districts.
- **County-level:** Cluj-Napoca hosts the prefecture (centrally appointed prefect, cannot be a party member) and county council headquarters.
- **Metropolitan area:** Since December 2008, encompasses the city plus 17 surrounding communes, population ~411,379.

[Source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cluj-Napoca; Source: primariaclujnapoca.ro]

**Civic tech-relevant institutions:**
- **Centrul de Inovare si Imaginatie Civica (CIIC):** Launched October 2017 by City Hall. Functions as a "permanent laboratory" for stakeholder collaboration. In 2022, integrated the Divizia de Inovare Urbana (DIU). 8 core activity pillars including public consultations, participatory governance, FIX Cluj (innovation grants), EU Climate Mission, SDG localization, Youth Advisory Council. [Source: primariaclujnapoca.ro/cetateni/centrul-de-inovare-si-imaginatie-civica/]
- **e-Primaria:** 300+ online procedures, digital citizen file, online payments, 140+ form templates. [Source: primariaclujnapoca.ro]
- **MyCluj app:** Citizen suggestion and complaint submission. [Source: primariaclujnapoca.ro]
- **Tichetul albastru:** Newer digital citizen communication channel. [Source: primariaclujnapoca.ro]
- **Transparency portal:** Public contracts, payments, asset declarations. [Source: primariaclujnapoca.ro]
- **GIS portal:** gis.primariaclujnapoca.ro -- spatial data access. [Source: primariaclujnapoca.ro]
- **Bugetare participativa platform:** bugetareparticipativa.ro -- citizen project proposals and voting. [Source: primariaclujnapoca.ro]
- **Digital transformation strategy (Strategia de transformare digitala):** Dedicated municipal digital strategy. [Source: primariaclujnapoca.ro]

**Key insight for content strategy:** Cluj-Napoca has one of the most developed civic tech ecosystems in Romania. The CIIC is a unique institution -- a civic innovation center embedded within City Hall. The UDMR's 4 council seats mean Hungarian-language civic engagement has formal political channels, but the question is whether digital tools serve Hungarian speakers equally.

**Confidence: HIGH** -- Authoritative sources (official municipal site, Wikipedia cross-referenced).

### 3.6 Cultural and Language Considerations for RO vs HU Audiences in Cluj-Napoca

**Demographic context:**
- Hungarians comprise 13.9% of Cluj-Napoca's population (2021 census), declining from 16.4% (2011) and 19.0% (2002). Historically the majority group until the 1960s.
- The city's Hungarian name is Kolozsvar. UDMR/RMDSZ holds 4 of 27 local council seats and maintains all departmental headquarters in Cluj.
- The Institute for Research on National Minorities (opened 2008) is based in Cluj.
- [Source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cluj-Napoca; Source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarians_in_Romania]

**Language rights framework:**
- Law 215/2002 permits use of minority languages in public administration where minorities exceed 20%. At 13.9%, Cluj-Napoca falls below this threshold, meaning official bilingual services are not legally required at the municipal level.
- Official documents are maintained "in Romanian only," per the Constitution. Hungarian organizations seek greater language rights.
- The Babes-Bolyai University (multilingual since 1959 merger of the Romanian Babes and Hungarian Bolyai universities) remains a contested issue -- Hungarian community seeks re-establishment of an independent Hungarian-language university.
- [Source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hungarians_in_Romania]

**Historical sensitivities:**
- The Funar era (1990s-2004): Mayor Gheorghe Funar's tenure was "marked by strong Romanian nationalism and acts of ethnic provocation against the Hungarian-speaking minority." This historical context means ethnic sensitivity in civic discourse is a live concern, not abstract.
- The 1956 Hungarian Revolution triggered student protests in Cluj, used by Romanian authorities as pretext to merge the Hungarian Bolyai University into the multilingual Babes-Bolyai.
- [Source: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cluj-Napoca]

**Implications for civic tech content:**

1. **Language accessibility is political:** For Hungarian-speaking audiences in Cluj, whether a civic tech tool supports Hungarian is itself a political statement. Writing about civic tech in Hungarian implicitly addresses the 20% threshold question -- digital tools could serve Hungarian speakers even where the law does not require it.

2. **Different institutional references:** Romanian audiences reference Primaria, Consiliul Local, ANSPDCP. Hungarian audiences reference RMDSZ, Kolozsvari Magyar Konventia, and Hungarian institutions across the border (K-Monitor, Atlatszo, TASZ). Content for HU audiences should acknowledge both Romanian institutional context and cross-border Hungarian civil society.

3. **Different threat models:** Romanian audiences worry about surveillance expansion, SLAPP suits, and inadequate whistleblower protection. Hungarian audiences (in Romania) worry about language rights erosion, demographic decline, and cultural autonomy. Hungarian audiences (in Hungary) face a far more acute threat landscape: Sovereignty Protection Office, Pegasus spyware, GDPR weaponization, media consolidation. Content for HU audience must distinguish between "Hungarian in Romania" and "Hungarian in Hungary" concerns.

4. **Different civic tech traditions:** Romania's civic tech scene (Code4ro, CIIC) is more institutionally integrated -- working with and within government. Hungary's civic tech scene (Atlatszo, K-Monitor, TASZ) is more oppositional -- building tools specifically because government transparency is failing. A Hungarian reader in Cluj may relate to both traditions.

5. **Identity layer:** For Hungarian speakers in Cluj, civic engagement carries a dual identity dimension -- participating in Romanian civic life while maintaining Hungarian community identity. Content that acknowledges this duality (e.g., using both Kolozsvar and Cluj-Napoca, referencing both RMDSZ and USR) will resonate more deeply.

6. **Content tone:** Romanian civic tech discourse tends toward institutional reform language ("digitalizare onesta," "transparency," "anti-corruption"). Hungarian civic tech discourse (especially from Hungary) is more urgent and oppositional ("defending against state overreach," "protecting civil society"). For HU audiences in Cluj, a tone that bridges institutional participation (Romanian model) with vigilance about rights (Hungarian model) would be appropriate.

**Confidence: MEDIUM-HIGH** -- Demographic and legal framework data is well-sourced. Cultural analysis is interpretive (labeled as such) based on cross-referencing of multiple sources.

## 4. Synthesis and Recommendations

### Overall Assessment

## 6. Conflicting Information

1. **SORM in Romania:** The original research question referenced "SORM" as a Romanian digital rights concern. Research confirms SORM is a Russian surveillance system (System for Operative Investigative Activities) with no documented connection to Romania. Romanian surveillance concerns center on Law 82/2012 (data retention) and intelligence service oversight, not SORM. **Resolution:** The SORM reference appears to be a conflation. Romanian digital rights concerns should be framed around data retention, intelligence oversight, and anti-SLAPP -- not SORM.

2. **PB implementation claims:** CRPE states 13 county capitals ran PB programs in 2022, while also stating 25 county capitals have council decisions obligating them. Wikipedia Romanian article lists Bucharest sectors 1, 2, 3, 6 as having PB portals. No direct contradiction, but the overlap between "cities with PB platforms" and "cities with active PB programs" is unclear. A city can have a portal (like bugetareparticipativa.primariasector2.ro) but may not have an active program. **Resolution:** Distinguish between "has PB infrastructure" (portal/ordinance) and "ran a PB cycle" (actual implementation). CRPE's 13-count figure is the more reliable metric for active implementation.

3. **Hungary Freedom House scores:** Freedom in the World gives Hungary 65/100 ("Partly Free"), while Nations in Transit gives 43/100 ("Transitional or Hybrid Regime"). These are different methodologies measuring different things, not a contradiction. Freedom on the Net gives 69/100 ("Partly Free"). **Resolution:** No conflict -- different indices measure different dimensions. For civic tech content, Freedom on the Net (69/100) is the most relevant.
