---
title: "What makes a civic tool useful before it is complete"
summary: "A civic product does not need to solve politics. It needs to help one group understand something, coordinate around it, and act."
date: 2025-05-15
lang: "en"
---

Civic software has a specific problem: the gap between "useful prototype" and "production system" is enormous, and the space in between is where most projects die.

The trap is thinking a civic tool needs to be complete before it matters. It does not. A civic tool is useful before it is complete when it does one or more of these things:

1. **Helps a group see something clearly.** A map of potholes, a list of budget items, a timeline of decisions — these make a situation legible.
2. **Lets people coordinate.** Not vote. Coordinate. Show support, surface ideas, find others who care about the same thing.
3. **Creates a record.** Public decisions, public proposals, public participation — these leave traces that can be referenced later.
4. **Reduces the cost of the next step.** A prototype does not need to replace a system. It needs to make the argument for the system easier to make.

The bar is not perfection. The bar is: does this help at least one group do something they could not do before, even imperfectly?

If yes, ship it. The polish can come later. The usefulness cannot wait.
