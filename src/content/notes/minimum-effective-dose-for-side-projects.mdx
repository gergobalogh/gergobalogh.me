---
title: "The minimum effective dose for side projects"
summary: "Not every project needs heroic effort. A small amount of consistent public work beats long private overthinking."
date: 2025-05-08
lang: "en"
---

Side projects die for two reasons: not enough time, and too much ambition. The first problem is real. The second is optional.

The minimum effective dose for a side project is smaller than you think. It is:

- **A working thing**, not a planned thing. Even a rough prototype that runs locally is more alive than a fully scoped design document.
- **A public thing**, not a private thing. Shipping to one person counts. Shipping to a URL counts more.
- **A finished-enough thing**, not a perfect thing. You can always add later. You cannot ship what you never finish.

Most side projects fail not because the idea was weak, but because the scope grew past what the maker could sustain. The antidote is to start smaller, ship sooner, and let the project tell you what it needs next — instead of deciding all of that before you begin.

Consistent small output beats heroic sprints. A note a month, a prototype a quarter, a project page that goes live before it feels ready — these compound.
