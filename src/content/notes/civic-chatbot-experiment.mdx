---
title: "A chatbot for the city: small experiments in civic AI"
summary: "What happens when you put 200K public records behind a read-only chatbot and let citizens ask questions in plain Romanian?"
date: 2025-05-13
lang: "en"
---

Cluj-Napoca publishes a lot of public data — over 200,000 citizen complaints, 16,000 council decisions, 19,000 construction permits, 145,000 local news articles. The data exists. Finding it does not.

Government portals sort by form fields and dropdowns. You need to know the right category, the right filter, the right button. A citizen with a question — "what are people complaining about on Calea Florești?" — is facing a search problem, not an information problem.

The chatbot is a small experiment in removing that gap. You type a question in Romanian. A language model figures out which datasets to query, with what parameters. It fetches the data, then generates a short cited answer. No accounts, no forms, no navigation.

The constraints are deliberate:

- **Read-only.** It cannot file complaints, pay taxes, or book appointments. It shows what is already public.
- **No personal data.** It refuses requests for CNPs, phone numbers, home addresses. No exceptions.
- **Cited answers.** Every factual claim links back to the source record. If the data is sparse or uncertain, it says so.
- **Evaluated, not guessed.** A 55-question test suite runs against it with audit-verified results. The gate passes at 96%+ before anything ships.

The approach is low-stakes on purpose. A chatbot that hallucinates a parking rule is annoying. A chatbot that hallucinates a legal right is dangerous. Keeping the scope to public read-only data, with explicit refusal on anything transactional, makes the worst-case failure tolerable.

Current status: the bot covers complaints, council decisions, construction permits, and local news. It runs, it is tested, and it is live. What it cannot do yet — real-time transit, air quality, complaint filing, voice — is Phase 2+ work that requires the information-only foundation to hold first.

The experiment is not "can AI answer questions about a city." The experiment is "can a small, constrained, evaluated system make public data genuinely more accessible than a portal." Early returns say yes.
