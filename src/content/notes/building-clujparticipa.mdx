clujparticipa is a prototype that lets residents of Cluj-Napoca submit proposals, show support for ideas, and place them on a map. It is not a voting system. It is a tool for surfacing what people might want before formal decisions are made.

Here is what I learned building the first version.

**The hardest part is not the tool — it is the social contract.** A participation platform implies that someone will listen. If proposals go into a void, the tool erodes trust instead of building it. I had to be explicit about what this is and is not: a coalition-building tool, not a production voting system. The qualifier is on every page because the temptation to over-promise is real.

**A map changes the conversation.** When proposals live on a map instead of in a list, people see their neighborhood differently. A complaint about a park in Mănăștur reads differently when you can see five other proposals within 500 meters. Geography creates adjacency, and adjacency creates the possibility of coalition — three people who did not know they wanted the same thing.

**The threshold problem is unavoidable.** Participatory budgeting in Romania is governed by local council decisions, not a national law. Twenty-five county capitals have committed to it; about half actually run programs, at roughly 0.2% of revenues. A tool cannot fix that gap. What it can do is make the gap visible — show what participation looks like when the political will is thin.

**Building for the city you have, not the city you want.** Cluj has a Civic Innovation Center inside City Hall, a participatory budgeting portal, an open data portal, and a digital transformation strategy. It also has a population where 14% speak Hungarian and most civic tech tools do not support their language. I made the first version in Romanian. That was the right call for launch. It is not the right call for the city.

### What I am watching

Now that people can use the tool, the questions shift from "does it work?" to "does it matter?" Specifically:

- **Do proposals cluster?** If most proposals land in the same two neighborhoods, the tool is amplifying already-engaged areas — not reaching new ones.
- **Do people build on each other's ideas?** The difference between a complaint funnel and a coalition tool is whether users react to existing proposals or only submit their own.
- **Does anything leave the platform?** A proposal that gets 50 supporters on clujparticipa and zero attention from the council is a data point. A proposal that gets 50 supporters and shows up in a council session is a different data point.
- **Who shows up?** If the user base matches Cluj's demographics, that is one thing. If it does not, the tool is participation theater for the already-connected.

I will write about what happens when I have enough signal to say something honest.
